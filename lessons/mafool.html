Mafʿūl — Object / Passive participle

الْمَفْعُول / اسْمُ الْمَفْعُول

Mafʿūl (الْمَفْعُول) is the object of the verb — what receives the action (or one of several related accusative roles after the verb).

Manṣūb (النَّصْبُ / مَنْصُوب) — the accusative case — is used for the objects of a verb. Textbooks often list five kinds of object (مَفَاعِيل):

a) Direct object — مَفْعُولٌ بِهِ

The thing or person directly affected by the verb (“what?” / “whom?”).

خَلَقَ اللَّهُ السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالْأَرْضَ — “Allah created the heavens and the earth.” (29:44)
وَقَتَلَ دَاوُودُ جَالُوتَ — “…and David slew Goliath.” (2:251)

b) Absolute object — مَفْعُولٌ مُطْلَقٌ

A verbal noun (مَصْدَر) from the same root as the verb, with tanwīn fatḥah (ً), placed after the verb to stress or specify the action (how much, how completely, etc.).

فَصَّلْنَاهُ تَفْصِيلًا — “We have set it forth in detail.” (17:12)
فَسَوْفَ يُحَاسَبُ حِسَابًا يَسِيرًا — “Then soon he will be called to account — an easy reckoning.” (84:8)

c) Object of time or place — مَفْعُولٌ فِيهِ

Accusative noun telling when or where the action happens.

سَيَعْلَمُونَ غَدًا — “They will know tomorrow.” (54:26)
وَنُدْخِلْكُمْ مُدْخَلًا كَرِيمًا — “…and We shall admit you to an honourable place.” (4:31)

Sample nouns of time (often accusative/adverbial in this sense):

لَيْلًاby night
نَهَارًاby day
يَوْمًا(for) a day
مَسَاءًin the evening

Sample nouns of place (often in adverbial / locative use):

فَوْقَabove
تَحْتَbelow
عِنْدَwith / at
لَدَىٰwith / near
أَمَامَin front of
وَرَاءَbehind

d) Object of purpose — مَفْعُولٌ لَهُ

Often a verbal noun with accusative vowelling (فَتْحَة — including tanwīn where appropriate) expressing aim, reason, or exchange (“in return for,” “so as to,” etc., by sense in English).

بَدَّلُوا نِعْمَتَ اللَّهِ كُفْرًا — “They exchanged Allah’s favour for ingratitude.” (14:28)
يُنْفِقُونَ أَمْوَالَهُمْ رِئَاءَ النَّاسِ — “They spend their wealth to be seen by people.” (4:38)

e) Object of accompaniment — مَفْعُولٌ مَعَهُ

Accusative noun meaning “with” the action or agent (what or who goes along with the verb).

فَأَجْمِعُوا أَمْرَكُمْ وَشُرَكَاءَكُمْ — “So resolve upon your plan, together with your associates.” (10:71)
قُوا أَنْفُسَكُمْ وَأَهْلِيكُمْ نَارًا — “Protect yourselves and your families from a Fire…” (66:6)

Word pattern: passive participle — اسْمُ الْمَفْعُول

The same term مَفْعُول also names the pattern اسْمُ الْمَفْعُول: “the one that is acted upon” — the passive participle (“-ed” in English).

Pattern from فَعَلَ

Prefix mīm + fatḥa (مَ) before the first root letter; insert wāw (و) after the second root letter (with the usual vowelling for the pattern).

فَعَلَ→مَفْعُول mafʿūl — done / “object” pattern
قَتَلَ→مَقْتُول maqtūl — killed
فَتَحَ→مَفْتُوح maftūḥ — opened
خَلَقَ→مَخْلُوق makhlūq — created

Declension (on the pattern مَفْعُول)

SingularDualPlural
Masc.مَفْعُولٌمَفْعُولَانِمَفْعُولُونَ
Fem.مَفْعُولَةٌمَفْعُولَتَانِمَفْعُولَاتٌ

Many vocabulary items follow this pattern, e.g. مَعْلُوم, مَحْرُوم, مَجْمُوع, مَشْهُود.

Transitive vs intransitive: A transitive verb (مُتَعَدٍّ) passes the action to an object — you can ask “what?” or “whom?” (كَتَبَ, قَتَلَ). An intransitive verb (لَازِم) does not take a direct object (قَعَدَ, خَرَجَ).
