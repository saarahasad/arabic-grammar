Kaana & its sisters

كَانَ وَ أَخَوَاتُهَا

Kaana and its sisters are incomplete verbs (الْأَفْعَالُ النَّاقِصَةُ — literally “deficient” verbs): they express the time or circumstance of a state, not the main “action” in the way a full verb does. They come before a nominal sentence (جُمْلَةٌ إِسْمِيَّةٌ).

Effect on the nominal sentence

When one of these verbs is added at the front:

  • The former مُبْتَدَأ is called اسْمُ كَانَ and stays marfūʿ (مَرْفُوع).
  • The former خَبَر is called خَبَرُ كَانَ and becomes manṣūb (مَنْصُوب).

This pattern is the opposite of إِنَّ and its sisters, where اسْمُ إِنَّ is manṣūb and خَبَرُ إِنَّ is marfūʿ.

Example

الْجَوُّ حَارٌ—The weather is hot.
كَانَ الْجَوُّ حَارًّا—The weather was hot.
  • الْجَوُّ: اسْمُ كَانَ marfūʿ.
  • حَارًّا: خَبَرُ كَانَ manṣūb (e.g. ـًا on the predicate).

Common verbs in this family

VerbMeaning (gloss)Sample

أَصْبَحَbecame / entered the morning as…أَصْبَحَ الْوَلَدُ سَعِيدًا

Other verbs (e.g. أَمْسَى, ظَلَّ, صَارَ, مَا زَالَ) belong to the same family in fuller grammars; they follow the same اسْمُ كَانَ / خَبَرُ كَانَ pattern.
