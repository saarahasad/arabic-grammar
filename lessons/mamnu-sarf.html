Mamnuʿ min al-ṣarf

الْمَمْنُوعُ مِنَ الصَّرْف

Arabic nouns are often grouped by how their endings change to show rafʿ, naṣb, and jarr. Session notes use three broad labels: mabnī (مَبْنِيّ), munṣarif / fully declinable (مُنْصَرِف), and mamnuʿ min al-ṣarf (مَمْنُوعٌ مِنَ الصَّرْف) — “blocked from full declension” (often taught as having only two distinct case-forms in the singular, and no kasrah or tanwīn on the last letter).

مَبْنِيّ — indeclinable

The word keeps the same shape regardless of grammatical role. Examples of word types that behave this way include demonstratives (أَسْمَاءُ الْإِشَارَة), relative nouns (الْأَسْمَاءُ الْمَوْصُولَة), and pronouns (الضَّمَائِر), e.g. هَذَا, ذَلِكَ, الَّذِي.

مُنْصَرِف — fully declinable (triptote)

Most nouns take three different endings for the three main cases, e.g. الْوَلَدُ / الْوَلَدَ / الْوَلَدِ (marfūʿ / manṣūb / majrūr).

مَمْنُوعٌ مِنَ الصَّرْف — diptote / “partly declinable”

These nouns do not take tanwīn, and they do not take kasrah on the last letter. In the singular they effectively show two shapes for the three cases: marfūʿ with damma, and manṣūb / majrūr both with fatḥah (so naṣb and jarr often look alike), e.g. إِبْرَاهِيمُ / إِبْرَاهِيمَ / إِبْرَاهِيمَ.

Common categories of مَمْنُوعٌ مِنَ الصَّرْف

  • Non-Arabic names — e.g. ثَمُودُ, فِرْعَوْنَ (when declined).
  • Some prophets’ names — e.g. إِبْرَاهِيمُ, يَعْقُوبُ, إِدْرِيسُ.

Prophetic names often not in this category (typically munṣarif): مُحَمَّدٌ, نُوحٌ, لُوطٌ, هُودٌ, شُعَيْبٌ, صَالِحٌ.

  • Names ending in ان (alif + nūn) — e.g. غَضْبَانُ, عُثْمَانُ.
  • Colour words — e.g. صَفْرَاءُ, أَحْمَرُ, أَسْوَدُ.
  • Some broken plurals — e.g. مَصَابِيحُ, مَسَاجِدُ.
  • Some Arabic women’s names — e.g. زَيْنَبُ, فَاطِمَةُ.
  • Some men’s names ending in ة (tāʾ marbūṭah) — e.g. حَمْزَةُ.
  • Angel names — e.g. جِبْرِيلُ.
  • Place names — e.g. مَدْيَنُ.

Terminology and lists vary slightly by textbook; use your course’s list with the Iʿrāb nodes for case endings in full sentences.
