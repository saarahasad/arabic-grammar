Prepositions

حُرُوفُ الْجَرِّ

Huruf al-Jarr (حُرُوفُ الْجَرِّ) are prepositions that are always followed by a noun or pronoun. They have their own meaning and exert a grammatical effect on what follows.

Classical grammars often count about seventeen “true” particles of jarr, including time/exception words that behave like them before a noun:

Grammatical Effect on Nouns

After these words, a following noun is typically majrūr (genitive): the ending moves toward كَسْرَة (kasrah ِ), e.g. مِنَ اللَّهِ from اللَّهُ.

Effect on Pronouns

Attached pronoun suffixes do not take the same “kasrah on the noun” change — they keep their shape, e.g. مِنْ + هُ = مِنْهُ. With بِ، فِي، عَلَى، إِلَى (and similar), the spelling adjusts when the preposition and suffix merge (e.g. عَلَيْهِ, إِلَيْكَ).

Special Rule: إِلَى and عَلَى with Pronouns

When إِلَى and عَلَى attach to pronouns, the final ى (alif maqṣūrah) becomes يْ (yāʾ with sukūn), e.g. إِلَيْكَ, عَلَيْهِ.

Special Rule: لِ with Pronouns

The preposition لِ usually becomes لَ before attached pronouns except first-person singular ـي: use لِي or emphatic لَيَّ (for me). Before other suffixes: لَهُ، لَهَا، لَهُمَا، لَهُمْ، لَهُنَّ، لَكَ، لَكِ، لَكُمَا، لَكُمْ، لَكُنَّ، لَنَا.
