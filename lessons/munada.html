The vocative

الْمُنَادَى

The vocative (الْمُنَادَى) is how you call or address someone — to get their attention or summon them. The person addressed is الْمُنَادَى (the “called” noun).

حُرُوفُ النِّدَاء — particles of address

  • يَا — “O …” (used for masculine and feminine).
  • يَا أَيُّهَا — “O …” with a following masculine noun that has الْ.
  • يَا أَيَّتُهَا — “O …” with a following feminine noun that has الْ.

When الْمُنَادَى is marfūʿ (مَرْفُوع)

  • Proper name after يَا — typically without tanwīn and without الْ, e.g. يَا زَيْدُ.
  • Intended common noun — you mean one specific person, e.g. يَا رَجُلُ (“O man!” to a particular man).
  • With الْ on the noun, use يَا أَيُّهَا / يَا أَيَّتُهَا, e.g. يَا أَيُّهَا الرَّجُلُ, يَا أَيَّتُهَا الْبِنْتُ.

When الْمُنَادَى is manṣūb (مَنْصُوب)

  • Mudāf — first part of an iḍāfah, e.g. يَا عَبْدَ اللَّهِ (from عَبْدُ اللَّهِ).
  • Unintended common noun — a general addressee (“anyone who fits”), e.g. يَا جَاهِلًا تَعَلَّمْ — “O ignorant one, learn!”
